NBA Player Value:
Performance Metrics & Salary Efficiency

Do NBA teams get what they pay for? This project explores the 2024-25 season to uncover who the top performers are, how stats vary by position, and which players deliver the most — or least — value per dollar.

Chapter 1

The Billion-Dollar Question

NBA teams collectively spend over $4 billion annually on player salaries. But are they getting proportional on-court performance for that investment?

Why This Matters

Not all high-paid players deliver proportional performance. A player earning $50M might produce less impact than one earning $5M. Understanding this gap is critical for team management, coaches, scouts, and fantasy analysts.

This project investigates that relationship across two phases: first understanding the performance landscape, then overlaying salary data to identify undervalued and overpaid players.

Chapter 3

Understanding the Performance Landscape

Before analyzing salary efficiency, we must first understand: Who are the top performers? How do stats vary by position and role?

Positional Scoring Breakdown

Point Guards (PG) lead all positions at 10.52 avg PPG, confirming their primary ball-handling and scoring role. Power Forwards rank second at 10.05 PPG — reflecting the evolution of the stretch big man in modern NBA. Centers have the lowest average at 8.63 PPG, as their role emphasizes interior defense and rebounding.

The gap between highest (PG: 10.52) and lowest (C: 8.63) is only ~1.9 points — scoring is more evenly distributed across positions than commonly assumed.

Beyond Averages: The Full Distribution

Bar charts only show averages. Box plots reveal the full story — range, outliers, and spread. PG has the highest median and widest spread: from bench players (3-4 PPG) to superstars (32+ PPG), showing the biggest performance gap of any position.

Centers show a tight cluster at lower values, with a few elite exceptions like Nikola Jokic (29.6 PPG) as clear outliers. Every position has significant variance — no position is uniformly high or low scoring.

Correlation Findings

PER correlates strongly with scoring ability (r = 0.72), high Usage Rate correlates with higher scoring (r = 0.68), and Assists correlate with team offensive efficiency (r = 0.56).

Interestingly, the most efficient players aren't always those with the highest usage — suggesting that ball-dominant players face diminishing returns on efficiency.

Chapter 4

The Money Question: Salary vs. Performance

Now we introduce the financial dimension. Which players deliver the best bang for the buck — and which teams spend wisely?

Methodology: Building the Value Model

We merged salary data with performance stats by normalizing player names and team codes. Players were filtered to rotation-level contributors: ≥40 games, ≥20 MPG, ≥$0.5M salary — yielding ~200 meaningful comparisons.

A degree-2 polynomial regression model predicts Expected PER from salary. The difference — Efficiency Surplus = Actual PER − Expected PER — reveals who outperforms or underperforms their contract.

# Salary tiers
def stier(m):
    if m >= 25: return 'Superstar (>=25M)'
    if m >= 15: return 'Star (15-25M)'
    if m >= 5:  return 'Role Player (5-15M)'
    return 'Minimum/Vet (<5M)'

# Value classification based on efficiency surplus
def vlabel(s):
    if s >= 4:    return 'STEAL'         # Massively outperforming contract
    if s >= 1.5:  return 'Undervalued'
    if s >= -1.5: return 'Fair Value'
    if s >= -4:   return 'Overpaid'
    return 'WAY Overpaid'      # Significantly underperforming

Chapter 5

Key Discoveries

The insights that emerge when performance data meets salary data — revealing the true value landscape of the NBA.

★

SGA: The League's Best Value

Shai Gilgeous-Alexander earns $35.9M but plays like a $60M+ player. His +12.01 Efficiency Surplus is the highest in the league — elite performance at a relative discount.

★

Rookie-Scale Goldmine

Players on rookie contracts (Mark Williams, Jalen Duren, Wembanyama) dominate the "STEAL" category. Young talent on cheap deals is the NBA's most reliable value source.

⚖

OKC: Smartest Spenders

Oklahoma City leads all teams with +2.22 average Efficiency Surplus. Their roster construction maximizes performance per dollar invested.

⚠

Washington: Worst ROI

The Wizards have the lowest team surplus at -2.05, suggesting systemic overpayment across their roster relative to on-court production.

⚙

Centers Dominate Value

7 of the top 10 "steals" play center — the position with the lowest average salary but consistently high PER, making them the league's most efficient investment.

⚠

Max Contracts ≠ Max Value

Several max-contract players (Beal, George, VanVleet) fall into "WAY Overpaid" territory, proving that the biggest contracts carry the biggest risk of negative surplus.

The Bottom Line

Higher salary does predict higher PER — but only weakly. The polynomial model explains limited variance (R² ≈ 0.25), meaning 75% of a player's efficiency is NOT explained by their salary. Teams that identify this gap early gain a significant competitive advantage.

The "sweet spot" for value is the $3M–$15M salary range, where many role players and young stars dramatically outperform expectations. The superstar tier ($25M+) is a high-risk, high-variance bet.
